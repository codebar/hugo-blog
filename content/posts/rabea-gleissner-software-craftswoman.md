Rabea is a software craftswoman at [8th Light](https://8thlight.com/), a regular coach at codebar, one of the organisers of London's [Ladies of Code](https://www.ladiesofcode.com/) and a keen [photographer](https://www.flickr.com/photos/rabeasfotos/).

**What did you want to be growing up?**

You know, I've often wondered about that myself! I'm not really sure I had any major desire to work in a particular job. I remember I enjoyed playing "office" with my friend and I created newspapers for my family to read, so maybe I can conclude that I was interested in journalism… I also liked photography (and I still do) and I think I would have liked to become a photographer but I always knew that it would be really difficult to make a decent living. My parents must have made that quite clear early on. Hahaha!

**When did your interest in tech start?**

I worked as a digital marketing manager for several years and managed website development projects. That meant I had worked with developers for ages. I always had a lot of respect for them because their work looked so complicated. I thought it would be amazing to have those skills but it didn't cross my mind that I could ever learn how to write code. That changed when one of my developer colleagues showed me a bit of HTML and CSS.

> I loved the satisfaction of changing a few lines of code and seeing the change on the website.

And it didn't seem to be rocket science after all! I learnt a bit more on Codecademy and then thought about doing a web development course.

**How did you make the transition to being a developer?**

I wasn't very happy in my old job. I felt like I was stuck in a hamster wheel, doing the same thing over and over without needing much creativity or brain power. In the end I was lucky enough to be selected for voluntary redundancy (yes, banking is a strange world!) which meant that I received a bit of a pay off. That helped me pay for the 12 week immersive coding bootcamp course at General Assembly.

I figured, if it didn't work out, I could always go back to marketing and I'd have had at least 12 weeks of using my brain. But the more weeks passed on that course, the more I knew that I didn't want to go back to my old job. I really, really enjoyed coding and was determined to find a job as a junior developer after the course.

**What was your first development job?**

After the coding bootcamp course I worked as a junior front end developer for a book publishing company. I was really grateful that I was given this opportunity because when you graduate from a bootcamp, you really don't know very much at all. So you have to find a team that is willing to train you up and has faith that you can learn quickly.

The team that I worked on included three other bootcamp graduates who were hired before me, so it was easy to slot in. They helped me a lot with getting up to speed. We implemented new features and fixed bugs for single page apps which were built with Backbone.js and received json data from various APIs.

It was fun but I didn't feel like I was learning enough. Especially coming from a bootcamp where you learn at such high speed and work 12 hour days, it was really strange to go back to a 9 to 5 job. And I missed using Ruby!

So I started to look around again and found the perfect opportunity for me: a software craftsmanship apprenticeship at 8th Light. During the apprenticeship I learnt how to write clean, high quality code in Ruby, Java and Elixir. And how to use TDD for everything. I had two experienced mentors who directed my learning and am rubbing shoulders with a lot of really good developers every day. I'm very lucky!

**What is your favourite thing about being a developer?**

There are so many great things about being a developer! Can I give you two? One is having the skills to build something.

> If I have an idea, I can just go and build it, put it online and the world can see it.

Another aspect I really enjoy is the satisfaction of solving problems. It's a great feeling to find a solution for something that I've been stuck on for ages.

**What is the coolest project you have worked on and why?**

I've only worked on practise projects during my apprenticeship, which meant that I wrote a Tic Tac Toe (Noughts & Crosses) game with an unbeatable computer player four times! So that wasn't overly exciting. However, I recently wrote an HTTP server in Java and then used it to serve a web version of my Java Tic Tac Toe game. That was cool.

**How did you get involved with codebar?**

I used to attend codebar as a student. And I told myself that once I had a job as a developer, I'd sign up and go as a coach. So that's what I did! My first coaching session was really hard. But it got easier over time and I really hope that I was able to help and encourage a lot of students.

**Why do you keep coming back to codebar?**

I'm passionate about bringing a more diverse group of people into the tech industry. Personally, I always felt that coding was something for boys who were really good at maths and physics. If you didn't excel at those subjects, then there was no point even thinking about learning how to code. Of course that is completely wrong! And I think if I'd had female role models, then I might have had the confidence to take the plunge earlier.

I also really like the codebar community. I know quite a lot of people there now, so when I go, it's nice to catch up with everyone.

**What are your plans for the future?**

I just graduated from my apprenticeship after nine months of learning. Phew! I feel like I'm finally starting to properly settle into my new career as a developer. I'm proud of how far I've come. If you had told me two years ago that future Rabea would be writing code for a living, I would have been very, very impressed with her.

Now I work for 8th Light as a software craftswoman, which means that I get to work on client projects and hopefully soon will be mentoring a new apprentice. At 8th Light we work on Ruby and Java projects but also Scala, Clojure, Swift and C#… any language or technology really. So it definitely keeps you on your toes and the learning never stops. Exactly what I want from a job!

**What advice would you give to aspiring developers?**

Don't get frustrated. Stick with it. Try and try and try. Bang your head against the wall. You will eventually figure it out. And: Google! Google everything. Most likely your problem has been resolved before and someone put it online. And don't think you need to memorise everything by heart. Every developer googles for things, no matter how experienced they are, so don't stress if you keep forgetting the syntax for something or forget a command. Google has the answer!
